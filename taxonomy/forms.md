# Forms  
A structured vocabulary of jewellery forms used in the VelvetMatter Ontology.  
Forms describe the spatial organisation, typological geometry, and functional intent of jewellery objects across global, historical, and contemporary design traditions.

Each form definition is expanded to support semantic clarity, metadata consistency, and machine‑learning applications.

---

### Anklet  
Jewellery worn around the ankle.  
A global form with deep cultural roots, typically composed of linked, rigid, or beaded structures. Used in both everyday and ceremonial contexts.

### Bangle  
Rigid bracelet form without a clasp.  
Characterised by a continuous circular or oval geometry. Common in South Asian, Middle Eastern, and modernist Nordic jewellery.

### Bracelet  
Jewellery worn around the wrist.  
Includes flexible chains, articulated links, hinged structures, and rigid forms. A foundational category in object classification.

### Brooch  
Pin‑based jewellery worn on clothing.  
Defined by a functional pin and clasp mechanism. Historically significant in Nordic, Celtic, and modernist design traditions.

### Chain  
Chain worn alone or with a pendant.  
A linear sequence of interconnected links forming a flexible strand. Serves as both an independent ornament and a structural support for pendants or charms.

### Charm  
Charm or small pendant for bracelet or chain.  
Miniature suspended elements used for personalisation, symbolism, or narrative expression.

### Cuff  
Open‑ended rigid bracelet form characterised by a wide band that encircles the wrist without a clasp.  
A sculptural form often associated with modernist metalwork.

### Cufflinks  
Fasteners for shirt cuffs.  
Functional jewellery combining mechanical precision with decorative surfaces.

### Earrings  
A jewellery form with an extensive global history, encompassing a wide typological range from compact, self‑contained units to elongated suspended compositions.  
Includes studs, hoops, drops, articulated forms, and sculptural constructions.

### Hair Comb  
A hair ornament defined by a crest‑like upper element and a linear arrangement of teeth forming a structured, planar silhouette.  
Used for both functional securing of hair and decorative display.

### Hairpin / Hair Stick  
Pin or stick designed to secure or decorate hair.  
Includes single‑pin, double‑pin, and sculptural variants across global traditions.

### Locket  
Pendant form featuring a hinged or opening compartment intended to hold a photograph or small keepsake.  
A sentimental form associated with memory, intimacy, and personal narrative.

### Medallion  
Large flat pendant form, often with relief or emblem.  
Used historically for commemorative, heraldic, or symbolic purposes.

### Necklace  
Jewellery worn around the neck.  
A broad category encompassing chains, rigid collars, articulated structures, and multi‑strand compositions.

### Pendant  
Object suspended from a chain or cord.  
Includes symbolic, sculptural, gem‑set, and functional forms. One of the most universal jewellery types.

### Pin  
Jewellery form with a straight pin, typically worn on clothing.  
Simpler than a brooch, often lacking a locking clasp. Includes stick pins and lapel pins.

### Ring  
Jewellery worn on the finger.  
A circular form with extensive typological diversity: bands, signets, sculptural rings, stone‑set rings, and ceremonial forms.

### Sculpture  
Three‑dimensional sculptural object.  
In VelvetMatter, this category includes wearable art, miniature sculpture, and jewellery‑adjacent objects that prioritise form over function.

### Set  
Coordinated set of jewellery pieces.  
A metadata grouping used for cataloguing ensembles such as necklace‑and‑earring sets or brooch‑and‑ring sets.

### Tiara  
Ornamental headpiece worn on the crown of the head, typically featuring an arched or semi‑circular form.  
Associated with ceremonial, bridal, and historical court jewellery.

### Tie Clip  
Clip used to secure a tie.  
A functional jewellery form combining mechanical tension with decorative surfaces.

### Tie Pin  
Pin‑style jewellery designed to secure or decorate a tie, typically featuring a straight needle‑like form.  
Historically significant in men’s jewellery of the 19th–20th centuries.
